The map looks like an answer key. It is not.

Geography offers constraints—mountain ranges that slow movement, rivers that carry commerce, coastlines that invite connection. Yet the map alone cannot explain why two neighboring states interpret the same terrain as an opportunity or a threat. The map is a set of **affordances**, and politics is the art of choosing which affordances become fate.

## Three quiet forces

1. **Distance is not neutral.** It shapes what is cheap, what is risky, and what is tempting.
2. **Chokepoints are psychological.** They produce stories about vulnerability and control.
3. **Frontiers are negotiated.** Borders are less like walls and more like ongoing arguments.

> A border is often the fossil of an old compromise.

If you read long enough, patterns appear: a port city that keeps returning as a prize, a mountain pass that becomes a recurring fear, a river that acts like an informal constitution for trade.

### A practical habit

When a new crisis breaks, I try to begin with a calm question: *What would still be true if the slogans were removed?* The answer is usually something boring—shipping lanes, energy grids, demography, doctrine. Boring is good. Boring tends to last.
