Institutions are not just rules; they are stories that have learned to repeat.

An organization’s “memory” is rarely a simple archive. It is closer to a set of **default moves**: what gets rewarded, what gets punished, what gets quietly ignored. Over time, these defaults become a kind of personality—stable enough to predict, flexible enough to deny.

## Remembering without reflection

Institutions remember through:

- **Procedures** that turn past decisions into present habits.
- **Metrics** that convert values into numbers (and then treat numbers as values).
- **Personnel** who carry informal knowledge from one era to the next.

And they forget through:

- Turnover.
- Reorganizations.
- Strategic ambiguity—when clear language would create accountability.

## A gentle warning

When people say an institution “learned its lesson,” I try to ask: *Which lesson, for whom, and at what layer?* A scandal may prompt a policy update while leaving incentives intact. The institution appears to change; the institution continues.

To explore institutions is to explore time: not what happened, but what gets preserved.
